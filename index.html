The Three Universal Coordination Principles

Cross-cultural research reveals that all effective ethical coordination, regardless of the specific moral tradition, relies on three organizational principles.

1

Capability-Based Connection

Coordination emerges from recognizing others' ethical capabilities, not from demanding shared beliefs. A utilitarian and a deontologist can work together by respecting each other's analytical strengths.

2

Temporal Coherence

Ethical decisions must integrate past wisdom, present reality, and future consequences. This ability to maintain coherence across time is a measurable predictor of ethical capability.

3

Cross-Scale Organization

The same architectural principles that enable individual moral agency also enable effective family, institutional, and societal ethics. Ethical systems are fractal.

Part III: The Measurement Toolkit

This framework integrates formal logic as a practical toolkit for ethical reasoning. Explore the different tools and their applications below.

Alternating-Time Temporal Logic (ATL)

For Strategic Ethical Analysis

ATL shifts the question from "Is this action right?" to "Do we have a strategy to ensure an ethical outcome?" It transforms abstract moral debate into practical analysis of capabilities and coordination.

Example: "Does our society have coalition strategies to guarantee universal healthcare access, regardless of political opposition or economic shifts?"

"Seeing To It That" Logic (STIT)

For Personal Moral Agency

STIT formalizes individual responsibility. It distinguishes between hoping for an outcome and having the actual capability to ethically ensure it happens. This prevents making commitments based on good intentions alone.

Example: It helps differentiate "I hope I'll be a good parent" (a wish) from "I have the skills and resources to ensure my child receives needed care" (a commitment based on capability).

Coalition Logic

For Distributed Ethical Responsibility

This tool is for problems no single person can solve. It models how groups can coordinate their capabilities to achieve ethical outcomes that are beyond any individual's power.

Example: "What combination of roles (HR, leadership, staff) and capabilities is needed to eliminate hiring bias, and how must they coordinate to succeed?"
